Analyzing Margot's Attempted Steal of Home

October 30, 2020

It’s World Series Game 5 with the series tied at 2. You’re standing on third with a teammate on first, you’re down by 1 in the bottom of the 4th with 2 outs, and future Hall of Famer Clayton Kershaw is on the mound. You’re a career 71% successful base stealer, and Kershaw’s back is to you as he brings his hands together above his head as he comes set. Do you steal home?

Well, that’s the question Manuel Margot asked himself, and you probably remember the result. Out. Inning over, Dodgers hold on to their 3-2 lead. It may’ve looked like a silly play in hindsight, but there’s more to it than that. Let’s dig in to numbers to see if the attempt was worth it. But first, clip here:

We will use run expectancy (RE) to determine if Margot’s attempt made sense. In baseball, we should typically make the decision that maximizes expected runs scored, as generally this is equivalent to maximizing expected probability of winning the game. The chart we will use is courtesy of Fangraphs, and is here:

If you don’t know how to read it, click here for the Fangraphs article.

First, we use our RE chart to take stock of the initial state. The chart tells us that with runners on first and third and 2 outs, we can expect to score 0.471 runs to the end of the inning. Now, we must calculate the REs for each possible outcome: a successful steal of home or unsuccessful steal of home. If successful, the end state is runner on first with 2 outs, which has a RE 0.214. But, a run scored (Margot to be specific), so our RE is 1 + 0.214 = 1.214. If unsuccessful, the inning is over and no run scores, yielding RE 0. Great, so the important REs are the following:

  1. Initial state: 0.471
  2. If successful: 1.214
  3. If unsuccessful: 0

Here comes the statistics piece. We are interested in the expected number of runs scored given an attempted steal of home; let’s call this quantity r. Now, let p = probability of successful attempt. By the Law of Total Expectation, we have r = 1.214p + 0(1-p). Since doing nothing (not attempting the steal) has RE 0.471, we should go if r = 1.214p + 0(1-p) > 0.471. Solving, we find we should go if p > 0.39, or 39%. Thus, if Margot thought he had a greater than 39% chance of success, the attempt was a good idea. Do I think Margot was trying to calculate this in his head as he stood on third? No, but he does know that (1) he is an above average runner and baserunner, (2) Kershaw is a lefty who takes a long time to come set, and (3) Turner was nowhere close to third for a potential pickoff. Given these variables, I think I like his decision to go. Moreover, since Kiermaier, the left-handed hitter at the plate, has bad splits against lefties (.288 career wOBA versus lefties), I like the decision even more.

Finally, I thought I’d do the same calculation but vary the number of outs. I won’t show the work, but this is what I found:

  • If 0 outs: Margot needs p > 0.98
  • If 1 out: Margot needs p > 0.73

I guess the story here is… thank goodness he went with 2 outs!

To conclude, note that although Margot was out, there was also the possibility that Kershaw might balk, advancing the runners. Stepping off and making a good throw home there is not an easy play, and there is no doubt Kershaw was far more focused on getting the batter out than monitoring Margot on third. It was a great play by Kershaw, great tag by Barnes, and great communication by first baseman Max Muncy to tell Kershaw to step off. Overall, it was a fascinating play in a high pressure spot in the World Series, and it doesn’t get any better than that.
